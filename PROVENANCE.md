# Provenance

## Purpose

Provenance is the record of origin, transformation, authorship, evidence, review, and revision.

Covenant Code requires provenance wherever a system creates, changes, interprets, publishes, relies on, or repairs meaningful information.

A system without provenance cannot reliably support truth, transparency, consent, living revision, accountability, or repair.

## Core Rule

Every meaningful claim, artifact, memory, decision, source request, AI output, human review, and repair action should be traceable enough to answer:

* Where did this come from?
* Who or what created it?
* When was it created?
* What changed?
* Why did it change?
* What evidence supports it?
* What uncertainty remains?
* Who reviewed it, if review occurred?
* What repair path exists if it is wrong?

## Provenance Categories

### Source Material

Original material provided by a user, client, contributor, witness, public source, private source, file, database, repository, or external record.

### User-Provided Content

Content intentionally supplied by a user, client, visitor, contributor, or administrator.

### AI-Generated Content

Content created, transformed, summarized, classified, inferred, drafted, scored, or recommended by an AI system.

AI-generated content is provenance for what the AI produced.

It is not primary evidence that the underlying claim is true.

### Human-Authored Content

Content created directly by a human author.

### Human-Reviewed Content

Content reviewed, approved, corrected, restricted, or rejected by a human reviewer.

### Inferred Claim

A claim derived from source material, analysis, pattern recognition, model output, or human interpretation.

An inferred claim must not be labeled as verified record unless independent verification exists.

### Symbolic Interpretation

Meaningful interpretation, metaphor, ritual, narrative, or symbolic framing.

Symbolic interpretation must not be confused with factual verification.

### Verified Record

A record supported by sufficient provenance, review, and evidence for its context.

Verified does not mean eternal or beyond revision.

## Required Provenance Fields

A provenance record should include:

* provenance id
* project id
* artifact id or claim id
* origin type
* source reference
* creator or actor role
* created at
* modified at
* transformation history
* AI involvement
* human review status
* evidence status
* confidence label
* consent status
* visibility status
* living revision status
* repair status
* notes

## Evidence Status

Use one of the following:

* none
* weak
* partial
* conflicting
* sufficient
* verified
* disputed
* retired

## Confidence Labels

Use one of the following:

* unknown
* low
* medium
* high
* verified

A high-confidence inference is not the same as verified record.

## Consent Status

Use one of the following:

* not_applicable
* granted
* changed
* withdrawn
* restricted
* unclear
* disputed

## Visibility Status

Use one of the following:

* public
* user_visible
* admin_only
* restricted
* protected
* retired

## Living Revision Status

Use one of the following:

* not_applicable
* current
* corrected
* annotated
* retracted
* expired
* restricted
* changed_consent
* updated_self_representation
* archived_for_accountability

## AI Provenance Standard

When AI is involved, record where appropriate:

* model or provider
* prompt or prompt category
* source inputs used
* output type
* confidence or uncertainty label
* human review status
* publication status
* correction status
* whether output is factual, inferential, symbolic, or draft content

AI output must be labeled as AI-involved.

AI output must not be treated as primary evidence for factual claims.

## Living Revision and Provenance

Provenance must preserve enough record truth to support accountability and repair.

It must also avoid freezing living people inside outdated memory.

When a record changes because a person, belief, project, relationship, consent state, or interpretation has changed, provenance should record:

* what changed
* why it changed
* who requested or approved the change
* what public-facing version changed
* whether prior content remains public, restricted, protected, or archived
* whether the old record should no longer be used as current representation

## Protective Privacy and Provenance

Privacy is a protective boundary.

Protected provenance may be restricted to authorized review.

Privacy must not be used to hide exploitation, false claims, missing sources, unclear ownership, or repair obligations.

The rule is:

Transparent by default.
Private where protection is required.
Auditable always.
Merciful with living people.

## Forbidden Provenance Patterns

Do not use provenance to:

* launder speculation into fact
* treat AI output as primary evidence
* hide missing sources
* expose protected people unnecessarily
* preserve outdated personal memory as current truth
* erase correction history
* conceal ownership changes
* disguise generated content as human-authored
* hide system failures
* create surveillance without purpose

## Completion Rule

A provenance record is complete when it provides enough context to support truth, transparency, consent, living revision, accountability, and repair without unnecessary exposure.
