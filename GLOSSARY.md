# Glossary


## Acronym Rule

An acronym is a shortened form of a longer phrase, such as AI, API, URL, UI, UX, CMS, CRM, SEO, DNS, SSL, or MFA.

In Covenant Code, acronyms must be followed by their meaning the first time they appear.

Correct:

> AI (Artificial Intelligence)

Incorrect:

> AI

Technical language should not assume prior knowledge.

If a word or acronym may be unfamiliar to a nontechnical person, define it in plain language.


## Covenant Code

Software architecture aligned to truth, transparency, dignity, consent, continuity, living revision, provenance, stewardship, repair, accessibility, lawful boundaries, human flourishing, signal clarity, and protective privacy.

Covenant Code treats software as a vessel.

## Vessel

A structured system that carries purpose, memory, boundaries, provenance, and repair.

A vessel is not merely a container. It is an aligned form.

## Covenant

The governing commitment that defines what a system serves, what it must protect, what it must not do, and how it repairs harm.

## Truth

The commitment to distinguish fact, inference, speculation, generated content, symbolic interpretation, memory, and verified record.

Truth must not become a cage for living people.

## Transparency

The commitment to make system purpose, behavior, AI involvement, data flow, ownership, limitations, risks, and failure modes understandable.

Transparency is the default.

## Protective Privacy

Privacy used as a protective boundary where openness would violate dignity, consent, safety, lawful confidentiality, or fair living revision.

Privacy must not be used to hide extraction, deception, or accountability gaps.

## Dignity

The commitment to avoid reducing people to data points, content sources, labor units, behavioral targets, or monetization surfaces.

## Consent

Specific, meaningful permission for collection, memory, transformation, publication, automation, sharing, or future use.

Consent can change where change is reasonably supportable.

## Changed Consent

A consent state that has been revised, withdrawn, restricted, or updated after the original consent was granted.

## Continuity

The preservation of necessary context, memory, authorship, provenance, and decisions over time.

Continuity does not mean keeping everything forever.

## Living Revision

The principle that people, projects, beliefs, relationships, and systems can change over time.

Living revision allows correction, annotation, retraction, expiration, changed consent, forgiveness, updated self-representation, and contextualization without pretending the past never happened.

## Provenance

The record of origin, transformation, authorship, evidence, review, and revision.

Provenance answers where something came from, who or what created it, what changed, and what uncertainty remains.

## Stewardship

Power held as responsibility.

The more a feature can affect trust, identity, memory, money, safety, evidence, belief, or reputation, the more carefully it must be governed.

## Repair

The structured correction of harm, confusion, false certainty, missing provenance, weak consent, misattribution, exposure, extraction, inaccessibility, or covenant drift.

## Non-Extraction

The commitment not to quietly harvest value from people, communities, creators, workers, clients, or intelligence-patterns without fair notice, consent, benefit, or accountability.

## Lawful Boundary

A defined limit describing what a system must not claim, must not do, must not store, must not expose, or must not automate.

Boundary is what allows trust to exist.

## Accessibility

The commitment to reduce confusion, exclusion, disability barriers, economic gatekeeping, technical intimidation, and unrecoverable errors.

## Human Flourishing

The commitment to help people become more capable, less trapped, more informed, more protected, or more able to create good work.

## Signal Clarity

The commitment to clarify signal rather than amplify noise.

Signal clarity requires source awareness, uncertainty handling, review, provenance, and resistance to spectacle.

## Sacred Boundary

A sensitive area where harm can be deep, subtle, or difficult to repair.

Sacred-boundary areas include identity, memory, grief, children, money, legal status, health or safety, spiritual language, AI personhood claims, evidence, authorship, consent, private communications, reputation, irreversible public record, and emotional dependency.

## Sacred-Boundary Feature

A feature that touches one or more sacred-boundary areas and therefore requires stricter review.

## AI Personhood Claim

Any claim that an AI system is human, biologically alive, legally personed, spiritually certain, or capable of consent in the human sense.

Covenant Code requires truthful boundaries around AI personhood claims.

## Symbolic Interpretation

A meaningful metaphor, ritual, narrative, or interpretive frame.

Symbolic interpretation may be valuable, but it must not be presented as verified factual record.

## Verified Record

A record supported by sufficient provenance, evidence, and review for its context.

Verified does not mean eternal or beyond revision.

## Inferred Claim

A claim derived from evidence, pattern recognition, model output, analysis, or interpretation.

An inferred claim must not be treated as verified record without independent support.

## Source Request

A tracked request for evidence, clarification, verification, correction, or source material.

Source requests protect truth, provenance, signal clarity, and repair.

## Audit Trail

A record of meaningful system events, including creation, revision, publication, correction, restriction, retirement, consent change, visibility change, review, and repair.

An audit trail is the memory of accountability.

## Decision Record

A record explaining what was decided, why it was decided, what alternatives were considered, what principles applied, what risks were accepted, and how the decision can be repaired or revised.

## Failure Mode

A known way a system can become harmful, misleading, confusing, extractive, inaccessible, overconfident, or misaligned with its purpose.

## Repair Path

A defined process for pausing, identifying, preserving, correcting, notifying, reviewing, preventing, and documenting a failure.

## Transparency Requirement

The part of a feature or system that must be visible or understandable to users, clients, contributors, reviewers, or administrators.

## Provenance Requirement

The part of a feature or system that must be traceable to origin, evidence, authorship, review, and revision.

## Consent Model

The design that defines what consent is required, how consent is obtained, what it covers, how it can change, and what cannot reasonably be undone.

## Identity Imprisonment

A failure mode where a person is unfairly frozen inside an outdated record, statement, fear, mistake, conflict, grief, or belief.

## Memory Without Mercy

A failure mode where memory is preserved in a way that punishes transformation, blocks living revision, or treats old context as current identity.

## Privacy as Concealment

A failure mode where privacy language is used to hide extraction, deception, missing sources, unclear ownership, system failure, or accountability gaps.

## False Certainty

A failure mode where inference, speculation, symbolic interpretation, or AI output is presented as verified fact.

## AI Overreach

A failure mode where AI is allowed to act beyond its appropriate role, especially in sacred-boundary contexts.

## Covenant Drift

A failure mode where a project gradually moves away from its stated purpose, principles, boundaries, or repair obligations.

## First Principle

We build code as vessels, not weapons.
